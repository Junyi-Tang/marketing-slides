A Topic-Based Segmentation Model for Identifying Segment-Level Drivers of Star Ratings

Sunghoon Kim, Sanghak Lee & Robert McCulloch
American Marketing Association · Journal of Marketing Research

A Bayesian mixture regression with an Ising model prior that simultaneously discovers latent segments, identifies groups of co-occurring words (topics) as satisfaction drivers, and estimates word-level impacts.

Motivation

Three Methodological Challenges

Analyzing unstructured text reviews for segmentation requires overcoming three obstacles simultaneously.

Challenge 1

Topic Identification

Individual words lack context. We need groups of frequently co-occurring words that form interpretable topics managers can act on.

Challenge 2

Dimensionality & Sparsity

Word frequency matrices are high-dimensional. Without efficient variable selection, traditional methods overfit or produce biased estimates.

Challenge 3

Heterogeneity

Reviewers have diverse reasons for ratings. Aggregate models miss segment-level differences in what drives satisfaction.

Model Framework

Latent Class Regression

Each observation belongs to one of $K$ latent segments, each with its own set of regression coefficients.

Eq. 1 — Segment-Level Regression
$$Y_i = \bar{X}_i^{\top} \beta_{H_i} + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \sim N(0,\,\sigma^2)$$
Plain English: Restaurant $i$'s star rating is a weighted sum of word frequencies. Which weights apply depends on the segment $H_i$ that restaurant $i$ belongs to. The error $\varepsilon_i$ captures unmodeled variation.
Eq. 2 — Segment Membership
$$\pi(H_i = k \mid \underline{d}) = d_k, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{K} d_k = 1$$
Plain English: Each restaurant is softly assigned to one of $K$ segments with probability $d_k$. The mixing proportions are learned from the data with a Dirichlet prior.
Why Latent Class?

Capturing Heterogeneity

Forcing one set of coefficients on all restaurants obscures real differences. The latent class framework lets the data reveal naturally occurring segments where different topics drive satisfaction.

Data Setup

Word Frequency Matrix

After preprocessing (stemming, stop-word removal, typo correction), each review becomes a vector of word counts. For restaurant segmentation, reviews of the same restaurant are aggregated into one document.

Variable Selection

Spike & Slab Prior

To handle high dimensionality, each word's coefficient is either "switched on" or "switched off" via a binary indicator $Z_{kp}$.

Eq. 3 — Spike & Slab Prior on $\beta_{kp}$
$$\pi(\beta_{kp} \mid Z_{kp}=1) = N(0,\,\tau_p^2) \qquad \pi(\beta_{kp}=0 \mid Z_{kp}=0) = 1$$
Plain English: A binary switch $Z_{kp}$ decides inclusion per word per segment. $Z_{kp}=1$ → the word is selected and gets a real coefficient ("slab"). $Z_{kp}=0$ → coefficient is exactly zero ("spike"), removing the word entirely.
Key Difference

Independent vs. Group Selection

Prior work selects each word independently. The proposed model selects groups of co-occurring words together via the Ising prior — producing interpretable topics, not isolated words.

Selection Criterion

Posterior Inclusion Probability

Selection is based on the proportion of MCMC iterations where $Z_{kp}=1$ (e.g., 87.7%), not on classical p-values. This is a fully Bayesian approach.

Core Innovation

The Ising Model Prior

The Ising prior creates dependence between selection indicators, so neighboring words (frequent co-occurrences) tend to be selected together as a topic.

Eq. 4 — Ising Distribution on $Z_k$
$$\pi(Z_k \mid \lambda) = \frac{1}{Q}\,\exp\!\Bigl(\lambda \sum_{p \sim \tilde{p}} w_{p\tilde{p}}\,Z_{kp}\,Z_{k\tilde{p}}\Bigr)$$
Plain English: Higher probability when neighboring word pairs ($p \sim \tilde{p}$, frequent co-occurrents) are both selected. $\lambda$ controls coupling strength; $w_{p\tilde{p}}$ weights by proximity; $Q$ is a normalizing constant ensuring the distribution sums to 1.
Eq. 5 — Conditional Selection Odds
$$\frac{P(Z_{kp}=1 \mid Z_{k[-p]})}{P(Z_{kp}=0 \mid Z_{k[-p]})} = \exp\!\Bigl[\lambda \sum_{p \sim \tilde{p}\mid k} w_{p\tilde{p}}\,I(Z_{k\tilde{p}}=1)\Bigr]$$
Plain English: The odds of selecting word $p$ grow exponentially with the weighted count of its already-selected neighbors. If none are selected, the sum is 0 and $\exp(0)=1$ (equal odds). The more neighbors selected, the stronger the pull.

Neighborhood Structure

Defining Word Neighbors

Before applying the Ising prior, we define which words are "neighbors" using Manhattan distance on binary occurrence vectors.

Eq. 6 — Manhattan Distance
$$\underline{\mathrm{dist}}_k(p,\,[-p]) = \sum_{i \in \mathrm{id}_k} \bigl|\dot{X}_{ip} - \dot{X}_{i[-p]}\bigr|$$
Plain English: $\dot{X}_{ip}=1$ if word $p$ appears in review $i$, else 0. Sum absolute differences across all reviews in segment $k$. Low distance = frequent co-occurrence = neighbors.
Threshold

Selecting Neighbors

Words with distance below the median are designated as neighbors. Sensitivity analysis shows robust results around this value. Smaller threshold → tighter topics; larger → broader topics.

Why Manhattan?

Design Choice

Manhattan distance is preferred for binary vectors — it simply counts differing occurrences. It is less affected by the curse of high dimensionality than Euclidean distance.

Tuning

Choosing $\lambda$

$\lambda$ is selected via log-marginal likelihood (Chib 1995). In the empirical study, $\lambda = 0.01$ was optimal. If $\lambda = 0$, the Ising prior reduces to independent selection with no grouping.

Estimation

MCMC Algorithm

The model is estimated via a Gibbs / Metropolis-Hastings sampler. After 10,000 burn-in iterations, 10,000 posterior draws are saved.

1

Preprocess

Clean texts, remove sparse words, build the word frequency matrix $\bar{X}$ (190 words).

2

Initialize

Set starting values for $\sigma^2$, $\underline{d}$, $H_i$, $\beta_k$, $Z_k$, $\tau^2$; prespecify $\lambda$.

3

Sample Segments

Draw segment assignments $H_i$ and update mixing proportions $\underline{d}$ from Dirichlet.

4

Joint Selection & Coefficients

Update neighbors, compute $w_{p\tilde{p}}$, sample $Z_{kp}$ (Bernoulli with Ising energy), then draw $\beta_{kp}$ if selected.

5

Variances

Draw $\tau_p^2$ and $\sigma^2$ from their inverse-Gamma full conditionals.

6

Relabel & Save

Fix label switching via relabeling algorithm. Save all posterior draws including neighbor structures.

Post-Hoc Analysis

Identifying Topics from MCMC Output

Saved neighbor structures are assembled into a symmetric word matrix per segment, then visualized with a hierarchical clustering heatmap.

Procedure

From Neighbors to Topics

1. Build symmetric word matrix From saved MCMC neighbor info, mark which words are neighbors of each other per segment.
2. Hierarchical clustering heatmap Apply hierarchical clustering. Darker rectangular blocks reveal groups of co-occurring words.
3. Interpret as topics Each cluster is interpreted as a meaningful topic (e.g., "ambience," "food quality," "staff friendliness").
Important Nuance

Word-Level Coefficients Within Topics

Even within a topic, individual words can have positive or negative coefficients. Co-occurrence does not imply the same direction of effect. This provides nuanced, actionable insight beyond coarse topic-level scores from traditional topic models.

“None of the reviewed methods identify segment-specific group variables influencing the dependent variable.” — Kim et al. (2024), p. 1135

Validation

Simulation Study: Perfect Topic Recovery

Simulated data: 1,000 restaurants, 3 segments, each with 3 true topics (5 co-occurring words each, 9 topics total). The proposed model perfectly recovered all true topics.

Proposed Model Result

Perfect Recovery

All 9 true topics (3 per segment) were recovered exactly. Hierarchical clustering dendrograms matched predefined word groups perfectly.

Robust across varying numbers of segments, sample sizes, predictor counts, and words-per-topic in Monte Carlo simulations (see Web Appendix).

Benchmark Failures

Why Existing Methods Fall Short

Unsupervised Topic Model (LDA) Mixed words across segments; identified aggregate-level topics that conflated segment-specific patterns. Failed to recover segment-specific topics.
Kim, Fong & DeSarbo (2012) Selected words independently per segment but provided no group structure whatsoever. Could not identify the true topics.

Contributions

Methodological & Managerial Impact

Methodological

First Unified Framework

Combines latent class regression, group variable selection, and the Ising model prior for unstructured text in a single Bayesian model.

Interpretability

Word-Level Nuance

Word-level coefficients within topics reveal nuanced effects — e.g., “kid” (−) vs. “fun” (+) within the same kid-friendly topic.
